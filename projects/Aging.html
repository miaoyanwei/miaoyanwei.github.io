A Literature Review On The Adoption Of Social Digital Technologies For Older Adults In Social Isolation

Maryam Akbarinigjeh, Yanwei Miao

Many countries have entered an ageing society. With the rapid development of science and technology, there has been a great deal of research on the social digital technologies, yet the relationship between older adults and these technologies is always neglected. The incapacity of older individuals to effectively use technological products leads them to be more socially isolated. Nowadays, the corona has made this phenomenon even more severe. Our review aimed to summarise the current literature on the social digital interventions for alleviating loneliness and social isolation among older people. This paper is organised as follow: First, we briefly discuss the fact that the world is currently ageing and how organisations around the world are taking this seriously. Next, we describe the methods we employed for conducting our literature review and data analysis. Some important terms are then summarised from the literature. After that, the current strategies and challenges that are introduced from the literature are therefore addressed. We also take note of the findings and opportunities from the literature. Finally, we draw a conclusion and acknowledge our limitations.

Introduction

In recent years, scholars from several academic disciplines have focused their attention on the importance of older people's roles and well-being in social structure. This is mostly caused by the steadily rising population of elderly individuals. The World Health Organization (WHO) estimates that by 2030, there will be 1.4 billion people in the world who are 60 or older, and this number is predicted to double by 2050. The key fact is that if the elderly are not provided with the appropriate care today, they will have a variety of problems in the future, and these issues will continue in the wave of global aging. Due to physical and financial problems, the elderly establish less communication with friends and people around them, which leads to a number of issues, including depression, loneliness, and social isolation [4, 5]. Every person's mental health is greatly influenced by their social relationships, and societies with more interpersonal communication will have greater psychological health. Telecommunications and Information Communication Technologies (ICTs) have a role to play in achieving healthier ageing and helping improve socioemotional outcomes. To focus on the critical role of ICT, the International Telecommunication Union (ITU) announced "Digital Technologies for Older People and Healthy Aging" as the theme of World Telecommunication and Information Society Day (WTISD) in the year 2022 [11].

Understanding loneliness & Social Isolation

Particular problems associated with aging which is thought to be critical determinants of well-being for older people are loneliness and social isolation [6]. Although loneliness and social isolation are closely related, they are studied as separate entities. A deeper comprehension of these concepts is needed to facilitate the development of ways to deal with them [5,6]. Some definitions that we discovered in the literature are presented in TABLE 2.

Term Ref Definition Factors associated
Loneliness [1] Loneliness is the distressing feeling experienced during perceived social isolation, akin to thirst or hunger pangs (Hawkley & Cacioppo, 2010). Loneliness varies in intensity, duration, and frequency (Barreto et al., 2021) and is usually distinguished between either a perceived lack in close relationships and feelings of emptiness (emotional loneliness), or a perceived lack of belonging to larger networks/- groups/communities (social loneliness) (Russell, Cutrona, Rose, & Yurko, 1984; Weiss, 1973, pp. xxii–236). Social isolation is by itself associated with poorer health outcomes; the psychological experience of loneliness makes an additional, unique contribution to illness and mortality. Loneliness predicts specific declines in physical, cognitive, and emotion regulation capabilities (Cacioppo & Hawkley, 2009). Loneliness in older people is a risk factor for cognitive decline, cardiovascular disease, reduced immunity, and other physical health problems (Cacioppo et al., 2015).
[2] Loneliness is a subjective condition which is related to the way people perceive, experience, and evaluate lack of communication with other people. Social loneliness is meant to identify the absence of a meaningful friendship, while emotional loneliness refers to the absence of an enduring intimate attachment to another. Emotional loneliness can be influenced only by forming intimate bonds, which may take longer time. Living arrangements and quality of social relationships highly affected the state of loneliness. Besides poor health status, some psychological conditions might be related to loneliness, including poor mental health, low self-efficacy beliefs, negative life events, and cognitive deficits. Social isolation, together with demographic, physical, psychological and economic risk factors that can contribute to its development.
[3] Loneliness refers to perceived social isolation or a subjective unpleasant and distressing feeling that results from a significant discrepancy or mismatch between one’s actual and desired social relationships. Social loneliness is characterized by the lack of engaging social networks, and emotional loneliness refers to the lack of close emotional attachment. Loneliness is associated with poorer cardiovascular health (Valtorta et al., 2018), lower cognitive function (Boss et al., 2015; Cacioppo et al., 2009), depression (Wang Et al., 2018); Courtin et al., 2017), anxiety, suicide ideation (Beutel et al., 2017), higher psychological distress (Richard et al., 2017; Paul et al., 2006), lower self-esteem (Jackson et al., 1991), sleep and stress problems (Choi et al., 2015; McHugh et al., 2013), and health behaviors such as lower physical activity (Pels et al., 2016).
[4] Loneliness is an aversive emotional state, which refers to the subjective judgment of perceiving oneself to be socially isolated (Heinrich & Gullone, 2006 ). It refers to a set of feelings that arise due to a difference between ideal and actual social relationships (Peplau, 1982 ). Loneliness has been associated with poorer physical and mental health in older adults, including cardiovascular disease, depression, anxiety, and risk of dementia. Loneliness may arise in response to events or transitions that significantly disrupt the individual’s social networks and relationships (e.g., bereavement, retirement, change in physical health and mobility), and which impact on the individual’s sense of identity and meaning in life (Morgan et al., 2020 ).
[5] Loneliness is understood as a subjective emotional state (Newall & Menec, 2017) Loneliness and social isolation are associated with one another (Shankar, McMunn, Demakakos, Hamer, & Steptoe, 2017), and are often related to greater feelings of psychological distress, symptoms of depression, and poor physical health (Alpert, 2017; Taylor, Taylor, Nguyen, & Chatters, 2016; van Beljouw et al., 2014). Factors associated with loneliness in older adults include social isolation, gender, ethnicity, socio-economic status, and poor mental health (Cohen-Mansfield, Hazan, Lerman, & Shalom, 2015).
[6] Loneliness is the subjective experience (Perlman D. 2004) of negative feelings about levels of social contact (Wenger GC, Burholt V 2004); otherwise stated, it is the involuntary state of social isolation or the feeling of being alone (Russell D. 2009) Loneliness does not stem solely from objective levels of contact, but rather results from the differences between the levels of desire for social relationships and the availability of relationships (Perlman D, Peplau LA. 1998). Researchers using loneliness measures typically ask respondents whether they feel lonely, whether they see enough of people, and whether they wish for more contact (Wenger GC, Burholt V 2004).
[7] There is agreement that loneliness is the subjective perception that intimate and social needs are not being met. Loneliness and social isolation are associated with reduced happiness, life satisfaction, and psychological well-being; increased depression; compromised physical and mental health; and higher mortality among older adults. Furthermore, these social-psychological contexts have been associated with lower access to health care services, and lower health care utilization in older age.
[8] loneliness refers to perceived feelings of lacking companionship and abandonment (Perissinotto et al., 2012). Older adults’ experiences of social isolation and loneliness negatively affect their health and well-being (Cornwell & Waite, 2009; Perissinotto et al., 2012; Steptoe, Shankar, Demakakos, & Wardle, 2013).
Social isolation [3] Social isolation describes an objective and a quantifiable aspect of social relationships. Social isolation is associated with lower self-rated physical health (Cornwell et al., 2009), lower health-related quality of life and health status (Hawton et al., 2011), worse cardiovascular and mental health (Leigh-Hunt et al., 2017), and increased vulnerability to dementia (Fratiglioni et al., 2000).
[4] Social isolation refers to an objective lack of social ties, as well as low levels of social support and participation (Cornwell & Waite, 2009 ). Late life loneliness and social isolation are highest in individuals who are not married orpartnered, who are bereaved, have lost a partner, have limited social networks and social activities, have poor self-perceived physical health, and are depressed (Dahlberg et al., 2021 ).
[5] Social isolation is often understood as an objective state relating to the number of social relationships or frequency of contact with others (Newall & Menec 2017) Newall and Menec ( Newall and Menec2017) argue that although one could experience loneliness without social isolation, or vice versa, researching both objective circumstances and subjective feelings together may provide a more holistic understanding of mental health, especially with respect to the outcomes of intervention studies.
[6] Social isolation is the objective experience (Perlman D. 2004) of the absence of contact with other people (Hawthorne G. 2006), especially the absence of contact with people who provide needed or desired social support (Wenger GC, Burholt V 2004). Although social isolation and loneliness are closely related concepts, the socially isolated person may not report feelings of loneliness even though they lack social contact (Havens B et al. 2004)(de Jong Gierveld J, Havens B.). Conversely, the person who is not socially isolated and has abundant social contact may report feelings of loneliness if that contact is not perceived as fulfilling what the person wants from the relationship (de Jong Gierveld J, Havens B. 2004).
[7] social isolation refers to a multifaceted objective condition in which there is an absence of social engagement and social connectedness within family, friendship and community social networks (Wister A et al. 2016) The influencing aspects of loneliness and social isolation on the effectiveness of technological interventions should also be noted.
[8] Social isolation refers to low/nonexistent levels of social support and participation and decreased quality/quantity of social ties (Cornwell & Waite, 2009) Social connectedness, which relates to quality rather than quantity of social interaction, seems to tackle feelings of loneliness and factors that contribute to social isolation.

Current Strategies

Current strategies to overcome loneliness and social isolation among older adults were demonstrated in the literature.

The strategies used by older people to manage loneliness are a combination of engaging with the social and physical world, cognitive coping strategies, and private strategies that they do not disclose to anyone [1].

Challenges

The adoption of technology for social connections among older adults demonstrates certain challenges, according to the literature.

Research on the association between older individuals' mental health and social technology use may be inconsistent due to a lack of clarity on how to define social communication outcomes and a lack of research on the types of technologies used for communication. This means that while some research indicates that older adults benefit from online social networking, other research findings are conflicting, particularly given the relative lack of studies focused on this subject. For instance, connecting with online communities may reduce time spent with family, or some older adults may be reluctant to use online platforms due to privacy concerns, lack of understanding of how to use them, and lack of user-friendly options for them [5].

Digital technology is reportedly less often used by older people to make new connections (Liddle et al., 2021), they primarily use technology to maintain in-person relationships. Furthermore, digital interventions are frequently considered to be ill-suited for older adults due to decreased internet access and lack of digital communication skills (Coelho & Duarte, 2016; Office for National Statistics, 2020). Moreover, while there is some evidence that older adults may have increased their usage of communicative technology, this may be linked to increased rather than decreased loneliness (Choi, Hammaker, DiNitto, & Marti, 2022; Holaday et al., 2021) [1].

Findings

After performing a comprehensive analyses of the retrieved reviews, we summed up the collected evidence in order to highlight the most important findings.

Quantitative and qualitative evidence is provided to support the function of ICT in alleviating social isolation among seniors, but a small number of participants included in these studies impedes the generalizability of findings, claim Stojanovic et al. [2]. They believe the success of technology in combating social isolation and loneliness is related to improvement of older people's communication with family and friends, as well as the possibility to easily access the news and other useful information.

Stuart et al. [1] believe that according to the Social Identity Approach to Health (SIAH), the key to reducing/mitigating loneliness is to help people find and sustain the group memberships that provide these psychological resources of meaning, connection, support, and agency. However, the challenge of technology adoption and continued use is one of the key challenges since attrition from digital technologies, including apps is often very high (Lazar et al., 2015).

Latikka et al. [3] reviewed 23 studies conducted from 2006 to the end of May 2021, and discovered the focus was on detection and prediction in less than half of the studies, whereas the alleviation of loneliness or social isolation was the focus in most of the considered studies. They also found that studies focusing on loneliness and social isolation alleviation, older adults have been introduced most often to social robots. However, none of the studies report the complete elimination of older adults’ loneliness experiences with the help of physical ICT, but they report success in alleviating them.

Opportunities

Stuart et al. [1] proposed 10 design guidelines when designing digital social interventions, which integrate psychological theories such as Social Identity Model of Identity Change (SIMIC) for maintaining well-being of older adults during life transitions, including age-related social restrictions, the health impacts of chronic loneliness, and restricted adoption of digital technology.

No. Guidelines
1 Design should be grounded in facilitating awareness of social groups, social goals, and strategies to enhancing collective or group activities.
2 Design needs to allow maintenance of multiple existing groups, ideally making multiple group memberships salient to the users.
3 Enable older adults to join/add new group memberships over time, to strengthen their social networks and provide resilience to social disruption or threats.
4 Enable self-report, active engagement with reporting and reflecting on identity, loneliness, and wellbeing.
5 Enable mutual social support mechanisms where they are desired (including both the giving and receiving of support) to enhance a feeling of reciprocity and self-worth.
6 Facilitate agency, by giving people control over their engagement and use of digital interventions. This also means giving people the option to separate groups from each other.
7 Help reduce the heightened social threat and anxiety accompanying chronic loneliness by supporting and scaffolding social interactions and enabling users to gradually build a positive sense of shared identity with new contacts.
8 Ensure that the intervention is non-stigmatising, and non-detrimental to wellbeing by heeding human rights principles in the development and deployment of the intervention, including consideration of accountability, equality, non-discrimination, accessibility and availability (Australian Rights Commission, 2021).
9 Create opportunities for older people to use digital technology to organise their own groups and engage in activism and awareness raising for social good, which could enhance agency and work towards reducing stigma (Liddle et al., 2021) and rebuilding communities after COVID-19.
10 Design must manage the burden of use that can be created by technology by using an interface suitable to older adults, and nudging reminders that suit the schedule and levels of technology use of older people.

Moreover, Stuart et al. [1] suggested to take these considerations in mind in further research on a digital loneliness intervention:

  • Understanding and predicting loneliness trends
  • Building for adoption and continued use
  • Integration of theory into interface and interaction design

The feasibility study of Barbosa Neves et al. [8] has also demonstrated that bringing together acceptability and efficacy, an accessible app can be a feasible tool for social connectedness if five feasibility elements are considered:

  • The active involvement for adoption of the app, learning dynamics, and type and continuity of use
  • The app’s perceived usefulness and functionality to be related to levels of acceptability and efficacy
  • The need for adjustment periods to learn to use the app even those with higher levels of digital literacy
  • Management of different intergenerational preferences, norms, and expectations
  • Having geographically distant relatives can amplify the app’s feasibility to enhance social connectedness

Conclusion

Overall, many methods show a promising research path for overcoming loneliness and social isolation. Some forms of physical ICT hold promising value in alleviating older adults’ loneliness; significant positive results were obtained in interventions using social robots, an ambient activity system that includes an activity sensor, and a smart home solution [3].

However, there is still a long way to go in order to address issues like the distrust that older adults have for technology, the frustrating ways of interaction that older people engage with it, and the fact that advanced technology is actually leaving them feeling even more lonely and isolated. The good news is that numerous scholars who are working to find solutions have also contributed a variety of suggestions.

In additon, to ensure that older people and their support networks are properly engaged in the design process, many papers raised that user-centred design (UCD) methodologies are vital for understanding and accommodating older adults' needs and the needs of their social groups/networks [1], therefore, interventions should engage older people as active participants and should involve older people in the process of planning, implementation and evaluation stages [2], and ICT accessibility requirements and standards must be considered from the design stage to ensure that products and services are accessible to all [4,8].

limitations

This review mainly refers to 8 papers due to time restrictions. Additionally, because we do not live in the same area, it was difficult for us to meet frequently throughout the semester break to discuss our findings. However, we appreciated the work and think the topic of an ageing society to be very meaningful, which we would like to explore further in the future.
